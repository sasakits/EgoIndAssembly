Abstract

Assessing procedural skill in industrial and medical assembly requires tracking how hands manipulate specific components over time—a capability we term fine-grained temporal hand reasoning. We introduce EgoIndAssembly, a diagnostic benchmark of 222 egocentric industrial and medical assembly videos that occupies a fundamentally harder regime than existing cooking/DIY benchmarks.

We formalize three diagnostic properties—semantic near-identity (P1), temporal hand-trajectory dependence (P2), and small discriminative regions (P3)—and conduct a baseline study spanning CLIP, hand-guided fusion, and VLM approaches. Our strongest baseline, Progressive Procedure Grounding (PPG), achieves 0.435 MoF (+73% over the best CLIP method), yet still misclassifies over half of all frames, quantifying the gap that must be closed before downstream skill assessment and feedback generation become viable.
